The thin blue lines you see when you open up the KiCAD PCB Editor are **ratlines**. They show which pins still need a copper trace between them based on your schematic. Every time you tweak the schematic, click the **Update PCB from Schematic** to refresh them.

<div class="gif-slot">
<img src="/guide/4.1.png"></img>
</div>

Hover over a pad and click <span class="inline-code">X</span> to route that net. As long as your schematic is right, you can trust the ratlines on where you should route your traces.

When a trace needs to go from the front to the back (or vice versa), you'll drop a **via** , a small plated hole connecting the layers. Use them, but don't go overboard (we shouldn't be seeing 40 vias on a two-sided board).

## Routing tips and conventions

- **No 90° trace corners.** Use 45s or curves instead , they're much friendlier to manufacture.
- **Keep angles obtuse, not acute** wherever a trace curves.
- **Limit vias.** Every one adds cost and a small amount of signal loss.
- **Keep signal traces on one layer where you can**.
- **Drop ground pours** on empty board space instead of leaving it bare copper, cleaner power, less noise.
- **Vary trace widths by current.** Thin traces are fine for signals; anything carrying real power (3v3, LED arrays) wants a noticeably wider trace.

## Silkscreen art

Once your routing's done, have some fun with the silkscreen (the yellow/pink layer used for text and art) on the front and back of your board. Add your name, your board's name, a picture of your dog, the Runtime logo, anything!

<div class="gif-slot">
<img src="/guide/4.2.png"></img>
</div>

Open the KiCAD **Image Converter**, drop an image logo in, and it'll give you a footprint you can place directly. You'll probably need to mess with the size to make it fit like how you want.
